# InTrades — 36 Pitfall Scenario Cards (Categorized by Suit Domain)

> **Suit Domains:** ♠️ Spades = Tools & Technology | ♥️ Hearts = Interpersonal & Customer Service | ♦️ Diamonds = Business Acumen | ♣️ Clubs = Safety, Compliance & Risk Management

## ♠️ Spades — Tools & Technology (9 cards)

| # | Scenario | Description |
|---|----------|-------------|
| 1 | The "Mental Note" Mistake | Failing to write down a client's specific request or a measurement, trusting memory instead, and inevitably getting it wrong during execution. |
| 2 | The Van Black Hole | Letting the work vehicle become so disorganized that 20% of billable time is wasted searching for tools or parts that are "somewhere in the back." |
| 3 | The Supply Run Drain | Starting the day without checking inventory, resulting in three separate trips to the supply house for minor items like screws or caulk. |
| 4 | The "I'll Do It Later" Logs | Failing to log hours or materials immediately after a job, leading to guesswork at the end of the week and lost revenue. |
| 5 | The Phantom Arrival | Telling a client "I'll be there between 8 and 12" and showing up at 11:55 AM without calling to give an ETA update. |
| 6 | Ignoring Manufacturer Specs | Assuming you know how a new material works (e.g., cure times or mixing ratios) without reading the technical data sheet. |
| 7 | The Site Cleanup Fail | Doing excellent technical work but leaving drywall dust, wire clippings, or footprints behind, which becomes the only thing the client remembers. |
| 8 | Battery Roulette | Arriving at a job site with cordless tools but no fully charged batteries and no charger in the truck. |
| 9 | Improvised Tool Use | Using a screwdriver as a chisel or a wrench as a hammer, damaging the tool and risking injury. |

## ♥️ Hearts — Interpersonal & Customer Service (9 cards)

| # | Scenario | Description |
|---|----------|-------------|
| 1 | The Jargon Barrier | Explaining a problem to a homeowner using strictly technical terms (e.g., "The delta-T is off on the plenum") causing confusion and mistrust. |
| 2 | The "Not My Job" Attitude | Refusing to help a team member move a heavy object or clean up because it falls outside your specific trade description. |
| 3 | Broadcasting Frustration | Swearing or venting about the project complications loud enough for the client (or their neighbors) to hear. |
| 4 | The Defensive Crouch | Reacting to a foreman's critique of your work with excuses rather than asking, "How can I do it better next time?" |
| 5 | Disrespecting the "Gatekeeper" | Being polite to the property owner but rude or dismissive to their assistant, spouse, or property manager. |
| 6 | The Smartphone Zombie | Being seen by the client scrolling social media or texting extensively while on the clock. |
| 7 | Over-Promising Timelines | Telling a client a job will take two days to make them happy, knowing deep down it requires four. |
| 8 | Ignoring Non-Verbal Cues | Continuing to talk or sell services when the client clearly looks rushed, annoyed, or uncomfortable. |
| 9 | Appearance Apathy | Arriving at an occupied residential job looking disheveled or smelling of strong odors (cigarettes, sweat, etc.), which impacts perceived professionalism. |

## ♦️ Diamonds — Business Acumen (9 cards)

| # | Scenario | Description |
|---|----------|-------------|
| 1 | The Revenue vs. Profit Trap | Confusing the check amount with personal income, forgetting to set aside money for taxes, insurance, and overhead. |
| 2 | Underbidding to Win | Dropping the price so low to get the job that you end up working for less than minimum wage once expenses are covered. |
| 3 | The "Handshake" Deal | Starting a substantial job without a signed contract or a deposit, leaving no recourse if the client refuses to pay. |
| 4 | Tool Debt | Buying the most expensive, top-tier brand tools on credit before the business has the cash flow to support the payments. |
| 5 | Receipt Shoeboxing | Throwing receipts on the dashboard and fading them in the sun, making tax season a nightmare and losing deductions. |
| 6 | Discounting Experience | Apologizing for your price when a client objects, rather than confidently explaining the value and expertise you provide. |
| 7 | Ignoring Vehicle Costs | Failing to factor in vehicle wear and tear, fuel, and insurance into the hourly or project rate. |
| 8 | Billing Surprise | Presenting a final invoice significantly higher than the estimate without having communicated the extra costs during the project. |
| 9 | Commingling Funds | Using the business debit card for personal lunch or gas for a personal vehicle, piercing the corporate veil and muddying accounting. |

## ♣️ Clubs — Safety, Compliance & Risk Management (9 cards)

| # | Scenario | Description |
|---|----------|-------------|
| 1 | The Guard Removal | Taking the safety guard off a grinder or saw because "it gets in the way," leading to a high risk of severe injury. |
| 2 | The "Hero" Lift | Attempting to lift a heavy boiler, beam, or unit alone to prove strength, resulting in a debilitating back injury. |
| 3 | The "Dead" Wire Assumption | Touching a wire or component assuming the breaker is off without testing it with a multimeter or voltage sniffer first. |
| 4 | PPE Fatigue | Removing safety glasses or ear protection because it's hot or uncomfortable "just for this one quick cut." |
| 5 | Code Guessing | Installing something based on "how we did it at the last company" rather than verifying the current local building codes. |
| 6 | Ladder Laziness | Using the top step of a stepladder or leaning a ladder at an unsafe angle to avoid going back to the truck for the right size. |
| 7 | Blind Cutting | Cutting into a wall or floor without checking for pipes, wires, or ducts behind the surface. |
| 8 | Ignoring Fatigue | Pushing through exhaustion to finish a job, which is statistically when the majority of severe technical errors and injuries occur. |
| 9 | Scope Creep Silence | Agreeing to "just fix this one other little thing" while on-site without documenting a change order or adjusting the timeline. |
